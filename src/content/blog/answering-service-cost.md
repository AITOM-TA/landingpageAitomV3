It's the first question everyone asks and almost nobody answers clearly: **how much does an answering service cost?** The market keeps things deliberately vague, with tiered price sheets and "starting at" numbers that mean nothing. Here are the real ranges you'll see in 2026, the billing traps to watch for, and how to compare a human service to an AI answering service.

## The three billing models

Before we talk numbers, you need to understand how you get billed. Three models coexist, and they don't compare directly.

- **Per call or per minute**: you pay for each call handled. Attractive on paper, unpredictable in reality: one busy month and the bill explodes.
- **Monthly plan**: a set number of calls included each month, then an overage charge beyond that. The most common model at answering services.
- **Flat-rate unlimited subscription**: a constant monthly amount no matter the volume. This is the AI answering service model.

Which model wins depends entirely on your volume and how much it swings. Pull your call log before you compare quotes; without it, every price sheet looks reasonable.

## The real ranges in 2026

For a human answering service, budget roughly (indicative US figures):

| Plan | Indicative price | What's included |
|---|---|---|
| Per-call billing | $1 to $2 / call | Simple message taking |
| Entry monthly plan | $30 to $130 / month | Limited minutes or calls included |
| Full monthly plan | $200 to $500 / month | Appointment scheduling, calendar |

Per-minute plans are also common and run roughly $0.70 to $1.50 per minute. These amounts cover **business hours**. After-hours, nights, weekends, and holidays push the bill up, often with a 30% to 50% premium.

## What drives answering service cost up or down

Two providers can quote figures three times apart for the same business. The gap usually comes down to five factors:

- **Coverage hours**: business hours only is the cheapest tier. True 24/7 with humans is the most expensive, because someone has to be paid at 3 a.m.
- **Volume and its swings**: on variable billing, every seasonal peak becomes a billing peak.
- **Script complexity**: taking a message is cheap. Qualifying the caller and booking into your calendar costs more, because it takes longer and requires training.
- **Industry requirements**: healthcare confidentiality rules and specially trained agents push prices up. We cover the specifics in our article on [medical answering services](/en/blog/medical-answering-service).
- **Language coverage**: bilingual agents typically carry a surcharge.

## The hidden costs to watch for

The advertised price is rarely the price you pay. Always check:

- **The setup fee**, sometimes charged once when you start.
- **The cost of overages**: what happens when you blow past your included call volume?
- **The hours actually covered**: "answering service" doesn't always mean 24/7.
- **The cancellation notice**, which can lock you in for several months.
- **Per-minute rounding**: a 61-second call billed as two minutes adds up quietly across hundreds of calls.
- **Billing for spam and wrong numbers**: on per-call plans, ask whether every pickup counts toward your bill.

## A worked example: 60 calls a day

Say you run a home services company that receives 60 calls a day. Your office team catches most of them, but 15 a day slip through: lunch breaks, peak mornings, evenings. That's about 330 missed calls a month you want a service to catch. As a rule of thumb, here is what the models look like:

- **Per call at $1.50**: about $495 a month. Fine, until a cold snap doubles your volume and the bill doubles with it.
- **Per minute at $1 with a 3-minute average**: about $990 a month, and the longer calls (usually the valuable ones) cost you the most.
- **Monthly plan at $250 for 250 calls**: works in a quiet month, then overage fees land exactly when you're busiest.
- **Flat AI subscription**: the same amount whether it takes 330 calls or every single one, day and night.

The numbers are hypothetical; the pattern is not. Variable pricing punishes your best months. Run the same math on your own call log before signing anything.

## Compared to an AI answering service

The AI answering service changes the cost logic on three points. First, the **fixed price**: you pay the same amount whether you receive 40 or 400 calls, so no nasty surprise at the end of the month. Second, **availability**: 24/7, with no night or weekend premium. Third, **unlimited simultaneous calls**: where a human service maxes out when three customers call at once, the AI takes them all.

| Cost dimension | Human answering service | AI answering service |
|---|---|---|
| Billing basis | Per call, per minute, or capped plan | Flat monthly subscription |
| Nights and weekends | Premium of 30% to 50%, when offered | Included |
| Three callers at once | Hold queue or voicemail | All three answered |
| Busy month | Bill goes up | Bill unchanged |
| Setup fee | Common | Usually none |

So the real basis for comparison isn't the raw price, but the **cost per call actually captured**. A cheap human service that only picks up during business hours lets every evening and weekend call slip through, often the most urgent ones. ## How to choose

Ask yourself three simple questions:

1. **What's my real call volume, and above all how much does it swing?** The more irregular it is, the more a flat monthly rate pays off.
2. **How many calls come in outside business hours?** If there are a lot, a service limited to office hours costs you dearly.
3. **Do I just need message taking, or real appointment scheduling?** The second case rules out entry-level plans right away.

If a human provider still fits your case best, our guide to [outsourced answering services](/en/blog/outsourced-answering-service) explains how to vet one. And keep in mind that price is only half the decision; the other half is what [professional call answering](/en/blog/professional-call-answering) requires on every single call, at 9 a.m. and at 9 p.m.

## The bottom line on answering service cost

Answering service cost is not the sticker price. It's the sticker price plus overages, after-hours premiums, and setup fees, minus nothing for the calls that still get missed. Divide each quote by the number of calls it will actually capture, on your real hours and your real volume, and the ranking of your options often flips.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much does an answering service cost per month?

As a rule of thumb, expect $30 to $130 a month for entry plans limited to message taking, and $200 to $500 for plans that include appointment scheduling. Per-call billing typically runs $1 to $2 per call, and per-minute billing $0.70 to $1.50. After-hours coverage usually adds a premium on top.

### Is an answering service cheaper than hiring a receptionist?

Almost always, for the phone alone. A full-time receptionist typically costs several thousand dollars a month once salary, taxes, and benefits are counted, and covers roughly 40 hours a week. The honest comparison also includes what a receptionist does beyond calls: greeting visitors, paperwork, scheduling support.

### Do answering services charge for spam calls?

Many do: when billing is per call or per minute, every pickup counts, including robocalls and wrong numbers. Ask explicitly how spam is filtered and whether it's billed. On a high-volume line, this line item alone can move the bill noticeably.

### How is an AI answering service priced?

Usually as a flat monthly subscription, with tiers based on features rather than call volume. Nights, weekends, and simultaneous calls are included instead of billed as extras. That's why comparing sticker prices with per-call services is misleading: the AI price is the whole price.
